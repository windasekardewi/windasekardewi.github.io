Grow sprint ownership to scrum team

By Winda Sekar Dewi

Background and purposes

Prepare sprint backlog

According to the Scrum Guide, there are two key points about the Sprint Backlog worth highlighting:

  1. The Sprint Backlog is a plan by and for the Developers.
  2. It is a highly visible, real-time picture of the work that the Developers plan to accomplish during the Sprint in order to achieve the Sprint Goal.

In practice, when running Sprint Planning, I often face situations where the sprint backlog is not ready yet. Based on the first point above, it should be planned by and for Developers. I suggest developers prepare tasks before the sprint planning meeting starts. It is easier for them to provide technical context, and it makes the sprint planning session more effective and focused.

For the second point, Developers are expected to sense how much work they can take on within a sprint (2 man weeks). In many cases, they tend to benchmark work against stakeholder expectations rather than their own capacity. They can and should state their own competency and capacity.

Set sprint goals

After deciding how much work they can take on, Developers are accountable for adapting their plan each day toward the Sprint Goal. Several variables can help Developers set meaningful sprint goals, and the TPM's role is to help them think through those variables, not decide for them.

Approach

As their TPM carrying a Scrum Master role, I serve the Scrum Team by coaching them in self-management and cross-functionality. Here is what that looks like in practice:

  1. At the start of sprint planning, show the timeline and scope of work to align on the target and surface any potential blockers.
  2. Give the team 20 minutes to create and prepare their tasks based on the initiative they are assigned to. Story points are useful but optional; if they need input from others, they can leave it blank.
  3. After tasks are added to the sprint, help the team assess how much work they can realistically take on:
    • Show the average story points they have actually completed in previous sprints.
    • Start a conversation by splitting the sprint: "What will you take on in week one? What about week two?" If the load still looks heavy, move to man-day projections: "How many days do you think this task will take?"
    • Factor in activities outside the sprint backlog: fast track, on-call duty, carried-over tasks, leave, context switching across initiatives.
    • Stay open to challenges. If a developer still wants to stretch and take on more, let them. Treat it as a fine-tuning moment to develop their own sense of capacity.
  4. When setting sprint goals, account for QA activities, targeted milestones, and dependencies, especially when another squad or tribe is waiting on a specific deliverable. Ask the PM to help order sprint goals by priority.
  5. Remind Developers that they are working as a team. Their teammates, TPM, and TL are all there to help remove blockers.
  6. Close the planning session with encouraging words. Something as simple as "Good luck for the sprint!" goes a long way.

Ideally, this facilitation is needed only once or twice. After that, the team should be able to run preparation independently before the sprint planning meeting.
