Program Management

Stakeholders management

By Winda Sekar Dewi · Learning from Stakeholders Management Training by Vicario Reinaldo, 21 July 2023

Tell the background

Everything needs a reason. The first step toward solving an impediment or introducing a new approach is understanding the reasons behind it. Stating an opinion without grounding it in context makes it much harder to gain alignment.

Share the alternatives

When you have experience in a given environment or product, you will start sensing when a decision may lead somewhere risky. When that happens, share your point of view backed by data, alongside alternative options. State the potential risks and mitigation strategies for each path, and let the decision-makers decide with full visibility.

Focus on what we can control

After sharing reasons, risks, and alternatives, let the decision-makers decide. This is a practice of stoicism at work — managing our focus by letting go of what we cannot control and directing energy toward what we can influence. It helps reduce the stress of working under pressure.

Deeply understand your key stakeholders

Stakeholders carry their own responsibilities and targets. Understanding them at a deeper level shows empathy and strengthens your communication. Assess stakeholders across four layers:

Motivation

Based on McClelland's Achievement Motivation Theory, motivation falls into three types:

  • Need for power — seeking influence, promotion, or a larger following within the organization
  • Need for achievement — creating new features, hitting targets, delivering measurable results
  • Need for affiliation — being liked, making popular decisions, avoiding conflict

Personality

The Big 5 personality traits are a useful lens:

  • Openness — curious, eager to learn, comfortable with change
  • Conscientiousness — organized, detail-minded, deadline-aware
  • Extraversion — energized by social settings, outgoing
  • Agreeableness — cooperative, easy to discuss with, open to persuasion
  • Neuroticism — prone to moodiness or emotional instability under pressure

Working style

Before starting a project or working with a new stakeholder, run a short 1-on-1 to align on:

  • Communication — synchronous or async? Frequent check-ins or document-first?
  • Timing — what windows work best, especially across time zones?
  • Strength and weakness — their technical depth, people management style, domain knowledge

Environment

Assess what is going on around them:

  • Personal — family, health, anything affecting their bandwidth
  • Organizational — internal pressures, new leadership, structural changes
Deep dive tip: Assess whether a situation is recent or long-standing. If it is new, the stakeholder may be dealing with something frustrating or disappointing — and that context shapes how they are showing up.

Generating buy-in from stakeholders

In negotiation, there are five steps to generate buy-in:

  1. Reach out accordingly — position yourself as a partner. Trust is built over time and makes your input more likely to be heard.
  2. Let them articulate their need — understand why they insist on a specific feature or date before pushing back.
  3. Validate your understanding:
    • Paraphrasing — "As far as I understand, you want to move this metric from X to Y, is that right?"
    • Blind spotting — share data and your hypothesis based on it
    • Appetite testing — "I understand you want X. Would you be interested in going 2X?"
  4. Propose your approach and allow them to respond:
    • If ready: offer options with pros and cons. Negotiate timelines with mitigation strategies. Consider chunking the release with a user communication plan.
    • If not ready: "Let me discuss with the team and get back to you by [date]." Always commit to an ETA.
  5. Before handling objections, assess the resistance:
    • Strength — how strong is the objection on a scale of 1 to 10?
    • Time — ask for a time box to collect data before responding
    • Method — what approach is most likely to work with this specific person?
Pro tip for remote teams: Use 1-on-1 video calls when navigating difficult conversations. Turning the camera on lets you observe expressions and read the room — something that is easy to miss in async communication.

Getting creative with difficult stakeholders

Classify stakeholders by criticality to manage your energy and communication approach:

  • Primary — directly involved: the project manager, project team members, and the sponsor (decision maker).
  • Secondary — indirectly involved. Keep them updated asynchronously.

Even after applying all the frameworks, you will encounter situations that go beyond theory — stakeholders confused about priorities, goals pulling in opposite directions, teams at risk of burnout from competing demands. Remember that as a TPM, your role is to bring clarity and act as a negotiator. No impediment is unsolvable, and there is always a way to communicate.
