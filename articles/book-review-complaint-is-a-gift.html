Book review: a complaint is a gift — the TPM perspective

By Winda Sekar Dewi · Based on A Complaint Is a Gift by Janelle Barlow and Claus Møller

A Complaint Is a Gift: Recovering Customer Loyalty When Things Go Wrong reframes the way businesses view customer complaints. Instead of treating them as negative feedback, Barlow and Møller argue that complaints are valuable opportunities to improve and strengthen relationships. Here is a summary of the key ideas — and how they apply to technical program management.

Key concepts from the book

Complaints as gifts

When a customer complains, they are providing free feedback on how the business can improve. The complaint represents a chance to recover loyalty and address issues that might otherwise go unnoticed.

Listening to complaints

Actively listening to dissatisfaction allows you to understand the root cause. Approaching complaints with empathy and a genuine desire to resolve the issue is what separates effective responses from defensive ones.

Responding effectively

Acknowledge the person's feelings, apologize sincerely, and take quick action. A well-handled complaint can actually increase loyalty — when people see that you are willing to take responsibility and make things right, trust deepens.

Building a complaint-friendly culture

Barlow and Møller advocate for environments where complaints are welcomed as opportunities for growth. This requires training people to handle feedback positively and building systems to track and learn from it.

Learning from complaints

Complaints highlight recurring issues. By analyzing them, organizations can identify systemic problems, make structural changes, and prevent similar issues in the future.

From the TPM perspective

Proactive issue resolution

Issues raised by stakeholders, team members, or clients can be treated as gifts — opportunities to fine-tune the program. Rather than seeing concerns as obstacles, a TPM can leverage them to improve project outcomes and build stronger trust with the team.

Active listening and empathy

When managing a program, actively listening to feedback from different teams and stakeholders is critical. Understanding the context behind a concern — and addressing it empathetically — prevents roadblocks and ensures everyone feels heard. That creates a collaborative environment instead of a defensive one.

Building trust through transparency

In project management, transparency is everything. When issues arise, openly communicating the challenges and the approach to resolving them strengthens stakeholder trust. Handling problems effectively builds your reputation as a reliable TPM — not just when things go well, but especially when they do not.

Creating a culture of continuous improvement

Creating an environment where team members feel safe raising issues and providing feedback leads to continuous improvement. As a TPM, fostering a culture where lessons learned are documented and applied in future phases is one of the highest-leverage things you can do.

Stakeholder management and satisfaction

Building an environment where stakeholders feel comfortable voicing concerns helps align project goals with their expectations. Regular check-ins, feedback loops, and genuine receptivity to concerns help manage satisfaction and prevent misalignment from compounding over time.

Turning problems into solutions

Problems in technical programs often surface weaknesses in processes, technology, or communication. By addressing these head-on rather than minimizing them, a TPM can turn blockers into opportunities to optimize the workflow and deliver better outcomes.

By applying the principles from A Complaint Is a Gift to technical program management, we can transform challenges into growth opportunities, strengthen team collaboration, and deliver programs that exceed expectations — not just meet them.
