How to manage a demotivated team

By Winda Sekar Dewi

I have been working in the tech industry for around 5 years — but it feels like 10. In a fast-paced environment where skills evolve quickly and every project gets checked daily, urgent incidents are resolved within hours, and the pressure rarely lets up. Team mental health often gets sidelined in pursuit of speed and output.

A study by the British Interactive Media Association found that people in the technology industry were five times more likely to experience depression than the general population, and that 52 percent had experienced anxiety or depression at some point. These numbers are not an abstraction — they show up in the teams we work with every day.

Remember that we are humans, not machines

Remote work makes it easy to forget that there is a person on the other side of the screen. We only hear voices in meetings, and some team members prefer the comment section over turning on their microphone. We lose sight of the fact that people need to eat, stretch, take a break, and step away from their screens.

Encouraging a short break every hour — even just five minutes — will not derail the work. It helps people sustain focus over the long run instead of burning out in the short term.

Encourage feedback and create safe spaces

Regularly seeking input from the team — on project status, new initiatives, or how things are going — is not just about information gathering. It is about meaningful dialogue. Create an environment where people can speak freely, knowing their concerns will be acknowledged, respected, and kept confidential where needed.

Surveys, retrospectives, and 1-on-1 sessions all serve this purpose. The goal is not to collect data — it is to make people feel heard.

Simplify scope by creating key milestones

Demotivated team members often report that their workload feels too large or too complex. Breaking large projects into smaller, clearly defined tasks helps address this directly. Smaller milestones give people more frequent experiences of completion, which matters more than it might seem.

Research on depression and motivation shows that diminished processing of rewards is a real factor. The more you can design the work to create visible wins, the more you reinforce a team member's belief that they can succeed at the next task too. Something as simple as marking a task done — publicly, in the sprint board — is an acknowledgment of effort and progress.

Share deadlines as needed, not all at once

A full project timeline can be overwhelming, especially for someone already under pressure. As a manager, you need visibility across the whole horizon — but that does not mean everyone does.

For team members who are struggling, share only the nearest deadline and let them focus on that. Breaking a large project into component parts and releasing deadlines incrementally reduces the input of stressors and helps people stay in motion rather than freezing up at the scale of what lies ahead.

Celebrate moments of achievement

Rather than spotlighting failures, focus on recognizing when people meet their commitments. Framing assignments in terms of their value and importance increases intrinsic motivation. Explaining why a task matters — not just what it is — helps people connect their work to something meaningful.

Celebrating small wins consistently does more for team morale over time than a single large recognition event. Make it a habit, not an exception.
